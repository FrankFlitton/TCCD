AFTERNoos SEssiox. The Speaker took the chair at 2:30. SCHOOLS.

Premier Haultain moved the second reading of the school assessment bill. He gave explanations of the provisions, stating that those of the present ordi­nance had been entirely rearranged. Provision was made for the obligatory use of the land tax system in rural school districts. Many clauses had been recast and many new ones had been added. The system in the rural school districts, was practically the same as in local improvement districts: the provisions for enforcement of pay­ment of taxes, and if necessary the final taking over of the land were the same. The forms provided were much more simple than the present ones, and also contained very full instruc­tions. While the House had agreed that the obligatory use of the land tax system in rural school districts was desirable, there might, at the same time be an exceptionally situated school in which this system would not suit. Provision was made for excep­tions whereby by order-in-council a school in any class might be declared to be a school of another class, if found necessary. There were ce n school districts, say in the municipality of Qu'Appelle, or of Indian Head, which were not villages, but had all the privi leges of town districts, and there was a provision by which such can be declar­ed to be town districts. The east Prince Albert school district was another exceptional district. The town of Prince Albert includes parts of three school districts, the public school district, the separate school dis­trict and the Prince Albert east dis­trict. It was proposed to deal with the last of these as a village school for the purposes of assessment and tax­ation, and for purposes of government and administration, holding meetings and electing trustees. SEED GRAIN IN THE N.W.T.

The Government Purchased it at the very Points Indicated by the Farmers Themselves.

OTT Awa, June 24th.-The attention of Mr. Smart, deputy minister of the interior, was called to an article ap­pearing in a Winnipeg paper of June 17, with reference to the seed grain supplied by the Dominion Government to farmers in Assiuiboia, in which criticism was made of the quality of the seed. Mr. Smart felt that it might appear necessary that some explana­tion should be given to show that the department endeavored to take such steps in connection with that matter as were necessary to provide proper seed for the farmers, whose crops last year had been so light as to leave them without a supply for this year. Mr. Smart stated, to begin with, that there did not appear to be any evidence whatever to show that the wheat which was sent to Mr. Horn and in­spected by him, was any of the wheat #. was delivered to farmers for seed. He said that it was found that in the work of iº. the seed at the dif­ferent points where it was, afterwards distributed to the farmers, it was neces­sary, owing to the limited time at the disposal of the department, to adopt the most expeditious way of providing for the best quality of seed without de­lay. It was decided to purchase all the seed wheat in the Territories and the main portion of it was purchased at Regina, Indian Head, Moose Jaw and Rosthern, and in small quantities at the smaller stations. The reason this was done was on account of an ob­jection which was urged against any seed being procured, except near the districts where it was required. It appeared that some of the farmers had strongly protested against any seed being sent in from Manitoba as they had an impression, no doubt quite an erroneous one, that the seed would certainly contain wild mustard seed or other seeds of a noxious character, and that serious consequences might result from introducing anything of this kind into the Territories. “In order to prevent anything but first class seed being purchased," Mr. Smart said, “the department sent a most practical man, Mr. C. W. Speers, the colonisation agent, to oversee the purchasing, and distribution of the seed. It is well known that Mr. Speers is a man of large experience in connection with grain and he made an examination and certified to all the grain that was bought in this connec­tion. I might point out that no com­plaints regarding the seed wheat have reached the department from any source, and it was with some surprise that I read the article referred to. “Some objection was taken to cer­tain seed oats supplied and the depart­ment immediately gave instructions that the seed, if found to be inferior, was not to be given out to the farmers at all, and some of the shipments sent in were afterwards sold.” “What did the Government pay for the seed wheat,” was asked. “The price paid by the Government in most cases for the seed wheat was from 73 to 75 cents, and the price charged to the farmer was, I thiuk, 76 cents in every case. The Epworth League.

Mr. J. W. Smith presided at the Epworth League on Monday last when there was a large and interesting meet­ing. After the usual devotional exer­cises, rev. Mr. Brown leading in prayer, the chairman called upon Mr. Wm. Trant to give an address on John Hunyan. The lecturer described the Puritan movement, not forgetting the coarse absurdities which characterised its adherents at one time, and which he accounted an intoxication from the must of the new wine of liberty of which they had drunkeu. Bunyan lived while the novement was at the height of its vigor, and possessed hut few traces of its intolerance. Though a Baptist he communed with Presbyterians and Independents, was the first man who dared to do so, and thus was the forerunner of the great Hobert Hall. Mr. Trant then sketched the life of Bunyan and described his writings. He concluded by saying that some years ago he had bimself witnessed the downtall of a great em­pire, and a few years later had seen a principality saved by the deposition of its ruler. He did not believe such disasters could happen if men and nations acted according to the political and religious ideas of the puritans. Here in this new country there was no empire in ruins: but the small be­ginnings of an empire about to be. It was the other end of the stick, and he thought the obvious duty of all was to do his utmost to build up this new nation on the principles ... Bunyan preached and for which Cromwell fought. At the close of the address a hearty vote of thanks was accorded Mr. Trant for his address on the inotion of Dr. Graham seconded by Mr. T. W. Peart. This was suitably acknowledged after which Mrs. Boyce, a sister of Mrs. Graham, sang a very pretty solo and saugit well, with grace and pre­cision. Regina, will be sorry to lose Mrs. Boyce, who will leave in a few days for Dawson City to join her hus­baud who is assistant Commissioner of the Yukon.
