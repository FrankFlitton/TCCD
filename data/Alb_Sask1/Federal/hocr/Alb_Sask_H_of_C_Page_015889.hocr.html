DEATH OF DR. JOHNSTON. M.P.

Sir WILFRID LAURIER. Mr. Speaker,
once again it is my very sad duty to have
to inform the House that we have lost by
death one of our colleagues. Dr. Johnston,
the member for West Lambton, is no more.
This is the fourth time during the present
session that death has visited the members
of this House. Nobody could have believed
when we met here some ﬁve months ago
that our late friend. Dr. Johnston, who then
appeared to be the very incarnation of
rugged health, would be one of the ﬁrst
victims; but such is the uncertain tenure
of life, that upon this, as upon many other
occasions, the hand of fate has been laid
upon him who looked to be the strongest.
I need not say to the House that Dr. John-

ston was one of the popular members
among us. He had no enemies; all his
acquaintances were his friends; he was

beloved by those on this side, and I know
he was equally respected by hon. gentle-
men opposite. If there was any place where
his high and sterling qualities were appre-
ciated more than elsewhere it was in the
town of ‘Sarnia and the county of West
Lambton, where he passed the greater por—
tion of his life. His death will be a sad
blow to his friends, above all to his family,
and I am sure that the members of this
House on both sides will unite in tendering
to his bereaved family the deep and sin-
cere expression of their sympathy.

Mr. R. L. BORDEX. Mr. Speaker, on-
behalf of those who sit at your left I join
with what has been said by the Prime Min-
ister. Our friend, Dr. Johnston, who has
passed away, was a man of very kindly
and genial disposition, and I cordially en-
dorse what the right hon. gentleman has
said as to the respect and esteem in which
he was held by those who sit on this side
of the House.
