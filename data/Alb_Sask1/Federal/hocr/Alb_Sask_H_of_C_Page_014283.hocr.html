For years, I might say for centuries,
there had been in England a system of na-
tional schools. But there were many sects,
many creeds in England, and it was felt
that it would be a violation of the principle
of religious liberty if the dissenters were
forced to send their children to schools,
the religious teaching of which was not in
conformity with their views. So, by the
side of national schools, grew up a system
of voluntary schools—that is to say, sep-
arate schools—where the dissenters of all
sects and denominations sent their child-
ren. The secular teaching did not differ
much from that of the national schools; but
the religious teaching given the children
was in accordance with the tenets of. the
parents’ faith. Catholics, Wesleyans, Quak-
ers established all over the realm voluntary
schools. So much so, that a time came
when more children frequented the volun-
tary schools than the national schools. The
board schools were state aided; the volun-
tary schools were self-sustaining. There
were in England, as there are in this coun-
try, partisans of the neutral school. There
were also men who thought that the child-
ren had a right to a religious teaching ac-
cording to the religious belief of their par-
ents.

Such was the opinion of Mr. Gladstone.
Such was able the opinion of Lord Salis-
