THE BOUNDARIES RESOLUTION.

Dr. Brett recollected an item of un­finished business. No motion had been proposed with a view to the transmission of Dr. Patrick's resolution on the ques tion of boundaries. The resolution had consumed much time; it had been ex­

haustively discussed, adopted and con­curred in. Why was it not to be trans mitted similarly to the resolution just adopted He had no doubt the matter had been merely overlooked, so he drew attention to it. Dr. Patrick said that as some members of the House, Dr. Brett included. had seemed to think that they had a right to deny the proposition that the people of the Territories had a right to deny the right of Parliament to alter the boundar­ies of the Territories without our consent, and as unanimity was essential to the proper effect of such a resolution the matter would be left where it was. v ARIOUS ORDINANCES BILL.

The House resolved itself into commit­tee on the bill to amend Various Ordi­

nanceS.

Dr. Patrick suggested an amendm nt

to the Magistrates Ordinance to provide for rooms for trials. At present there

was no provision for the renting of His suggestion was that J.P.'s

rooms. be permitted to add say $1.00 to the item

of “costs" to be devoted to the cºst of

hiring the room. Mr. Haultain said the prop sal would

open up a very large question, and , the

suggested provision was one for which there had been practically no demand. Only twice within his recollection had there been applications or complaints in this particular. To adopt the suggestion would open the door for a large drain

upon the general revenue fund, because

the inflict on of “costs” in magistrates' trials was by no means the rule. No costs could be levied where cases were dismissed, and sometimes convicted per­sons elected to take imprisonment as an alternative to the fine and costs. If they opened the door, the Government would be obliged to pay the rental in cases where costs were not levied, and the Government was not disposed to take the responsibility until the necessity became more apparent than it seemed to be yet. Mr. Haultain did not think there was much call for the amendment. A great majority of the magistrates held their trials at their own houses. The position of magistrates was largely hºnorary ; they got practically ne remuneration for their services, nor was it the intention that they should be paid for their services The class of men fitted for the duties was a class of men who were willing to fulfil the duties imposed by the position with­out financial recompense, and the great majority ºf the magistrates did provide, and willingly provided, their own rooms for trials.

The proposal was dropped ; the bill was reported, read the third time and passed; and Mr. Speaker left the Chair until 2:30 o'clock.
