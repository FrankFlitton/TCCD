The Public Health, Law Society and Medical Bills were passed through com­mittee stage. PRINCE ALBERT SCHOOLS. In committee on the School law amend­ments, in the afternoon, there was a pro tracted and somewhat agrimonious dis­cussion upon an involved question, affect­ing the Prince Albert school districts. The town of Prince Albert together with a considerable area of surrounding coun: try comprises two school districts. The ordinary elections of the ordinary Town school districts are conducted at the same time as municipal elections and by the municipal machinery. The Prince Albert school districts have not come under these provisions, but some years ago, after a great deal of trouble and con­sideration, the Assembly framed some special provisions to cover the cases at Prince Albert. Mr. Agnew wanted those special provisions abrogated and others inserted. A money by-law, recently de­feated in one of the districts, is to be sub mitted in amended form. Mr. Agnew confessed that he had assisted in defeat­ing the first by-law by getting in the voters who live outside the town proper. but belong to the district, and who in re­ality have not sufficient property interest to morally entitle them to vote. He favors the by-law to be submitted, and wanted to guard against the possibility of such voters defeating it. He therefore wanted a property qualification imposed. He wanted also provision for a ballot vote, and for the voting day to be lunger than the two hours of country school districts. Mr. Haultain drafted provisions to cover the latter points, without abrogat ing the special provisions already passed for the benefit of the districts. Mr. Betts could see no necessity for repealing those provisions at this time. It came out in the discussion that attempts have been made to join the districts, and have one district operated as an ordinary Town School District. Mr. Agnew was con­fident that the union would never take place, while Mr. Speaker seemed to hope that the union was by no means impos­sible. The matter occupied the larger part of the afternoon and was left over for further consideration. An amendment was inserted to enable the trustees of any yearly school to grant an extra two weeks' vacation. Hereto­fore the permission has applied only to town schools.

OTHER AMENDMENTS.

The Ordinance was further amended to include in the list of property exempt­ed from school taxation, grain for six months after threshed if still the property of the grower. Mr. Haultain explained that the principle was to tax no grain as against the grower. Cases had arisen where farmers had grain stored in eleva­tors within a school district; the grain was assessed, of course quite legally and properly ; but in reality it had never been the intention to tax grain. Even if the tax was put on the grain dealer, the probability was that the dealer would arrange matters so that the tax, and interest and commission on the tax, would still fall upon the settler. To put on a tax would give the dealer a chance to lessen the price by more than the amount of the tax. It would be similar to the old freight rate argument. A half cent. per hundred weight addition­al rata, seemed to give the right to add 3 or 4 cents a lb. to the price of an ar­tiele.

The bill was read the third time and passed. WArioUS ORDINANCES.

The bill amending various Ordinances was taken up in committee.

An alteration was made in a section of the Public Works Ordinance relating to control by the commissioner over navi­gable streams. The Minister of Justice had pointed out that jurisdiction rests at Ottawa. The section was amended ac­cordingly.

THE GAME LAWS.

Change was made making the season for snipe and sandpiper the same as for ducks.

The section respecting close season for ducks was amended in a way to prohibit spring shooting. Certain of the northern members opposed the change, but the ma jority was against them.

On motion of Dr. Patrick the provision for the granting of free guest licenses was abrogated; and a new clause was added to prevent non residents being granted the license to shoot until two weeks after the end of the close season.

AGRICULTURAL SOCIETIES.

The number of members to entitle to the grant was reduced from 100 to 50. Mr. Ross explained that the number had been raised last year with the object of discouraging the multiplicity of small shows. However the scheme which the Government were attempting to frame, and which would be introduced at next session, if the Government be sustained at the polls, contemplated the utilization of the small societies with the aim of hold­ing larger district shows, and therefore it was not desirable to continue the high minimum membership which might force disorganisation in many cases.
