But, when we are dealing with half a mil- lion square miles of territory of our North- west, we must not forget that time is ﬂeet- ing, that history moves on, and that nowa- days great changes take place within a few years. We have often been referred to the year 1875 in this debate, the year when the law was passed for the regulation of the Northwest, when the school law was placed upon the statute-book. Who would have expected at that time that people to—day would be spending time in discussing the school regulations for a country in which, I understand, there were in 1875, only about 500 people. We know that that part of the country was then regarded as a part that would not attain anything like the im- portance it has attained to-day. Within a quarter of a century, since the building of the Canadian Paciﬁc Railway, wonderful changes have come over that part of our country. Immigration is pouring in and development is rapidly progressing. Not only are the eyes of the old provinces ﬁxed on the Northwest, but the eyes of the em- pire are upon that country as the future granary of the empire.
