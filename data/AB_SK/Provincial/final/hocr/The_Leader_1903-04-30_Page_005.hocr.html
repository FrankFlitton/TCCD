THURSDAY, April 23rd.

The session of the Assembly thisafternoon was spent in the considera-tion of a large number of Bills, by farthe most important of which was theBill to amend the Liquor LicenceOrdinance introduced by PremierHaultain. Mr. Thos. W. Taylor, M.P.P.for Centre of Winnipeg occupied a seaton the floor of the House during Mr.Haultain's brief description of the objects of this Bill.

ELECTION OF DEPUTY SPEAKER.

After Mr. Speaker had taken thechair the Premier moved the appoint-ment of Alexander Cameton Ruther-ford (Stratheona) as Deputy-Speaker ofthe House. The nomination was hearti-ly endorsed by Mr. R. B. Bennett,both speakers joining in testifying tothe eminent fitness of Mr. Rutherfordfor the position, which, by reason ofthe fact that much of the actual busi-ness of the House was transacted inCommittee, was a most important one.

THE LIQUOR LICENCE ORDINANCE.

In moving the second reading of theBill to amend the Liquor Licence Or-dinance, Premier Haultain said theintention of the Bill was to increasethe requirements of hotel premisesentitled to receive licences and makethem more efficient for the purposesfor which they are operated. Thestandard of buildings and their equip-ment entitled to receive licences was,therefore, raised, and hotels in cities,and towns would have required to have



at least 20 bedrooms for the use of the public, and in villages ten bedrooms.

NO BEER ON RACE TRACKS.

The bill also does away with the per-mits to sell beer on race tracks. Thispower, said the Premier, was limited afew years ago by doing away with the privilege in connection with races atagricultural shows but it was now pro-posed to go further and do away withit altogether. There was alwaystrouble in connection with the issue ofthese permits and they were invariably abused.

NO LICENCES TO MARRIED WOMEN.

In future no licences would be grant-ed to a marriage woman that is to alady whose husband is living. A diffi-culty had been met with in the case ofmen who had forfeited their licencesecuring others in the name of theirwives. It was to put a stop to this practice.

NEED OF BETTER HOTEL ACCOMODA-TION.

The proposition to increase the hotel accommodation was one Mr. Haultainsaid he thought should be generallyapproved. The hotel accommodationat the present time was quite insuffic-ient for the present demands of thetravelling public and the business wasincreasing to such an extent that theywere quite justified in raising the standard.

LICENCES CONFINED TO CITIES, TOWNSAND VILLAGES.

After this licence years, the Premiercontinued, is was proposed to restrictlicences to cities, towns, and villages.This would affect certain hotels incountry places which are more likestopping places than hotels. Theywere given a year, however, before thechange would be made and manypoints at which hotels might reason-ably be established and conductedwould have arrived at the village stage. The less liquor at what might be term-ed simply stopping places the better.

There are also some important pro-visions for the simplification of theLiquor Licence Ordinance with refer-ence to the obtaining of convictionsand in some cases the penalties are in-creased.

A PROHIBITORY CLAUSE.

One clause gives to the LieutenantGovernor in Council power by procla-mation to bring into force the pro-visions of the prohibitory clauses ofthe old N.W.T Act. In this connec-tion Mr. Haultain said there were someportions of the country where it was impossible to extend the licence systemand where consequently there werepractically no restrictions now and where the provisions of the old pro-hibitory law might with advantage beenforced and it was for this reason thatthey were asking for power to proclaimsuch provisions by order-in-council.

BREWERIES TO BE LICENCED.

They were also asking that brewer-ies and distilleries be licenced. Atpresent there were not distilleries in theTerritories but there were a number ofbreweries. Some of these were a creditto the Territories ; others were the re-verse. It was only right that brewer-ies should pay a licence the same as allother dealers in intoxicating liquors.

AN IMPORTANT DEPARTURE.

Another important and new provis-ion provides that not only the manholding the licence but also the prem-ises may be disqualified under certainconditions for violations of the law. So long as landlords are willing to leasetheir premises without regard to thecharacter of the lesser then they shouldsuffer the penalty for infractions ofthe law. In conclusion Mr. Haultainsaid that it was proposed to ask for the second reading of the Bill, have itprinted and distributed and consideredby the Members and the country dur-ing the adjournment of the House sothat all would be in a position to deal with it when the House reassembles inJune.

OPPOSITION'S MILD CRITICISM.

Mr. R. B. Bennett said there was nolegislation on the statute book aboutwhich there was a more uniform desireon the part of the House to see enforc-ed than the liquor legislation. So faras the general provision of the legisla-tion now proposed, that is, to provide for better accommodation in the coun-try for the travelling public and to en-froce the authority of the Assumblywith respect to the liquor traffic, therewould be general agreement. In viewof the poor indoor-or rather outdooraccommodation-for the travellingpublic he thought the Legislatureshould pass the most drastic legislationto secure to the people some comfortat least. The hotels are making greatsums of money and ought to provide

good accommodation. He was alsostrongly in favor of restricting thegranting of licences to hotels in settledcommunities. Anything to do awaywith the liquor at mere stopping placeswhere people on their way to and frommarket towns wasted their money forliquor would be endorsed. As a gen-eral rule he was not in favor of legisla-tion which empowered the LieutenantGovernor in Council to act in the capa-city of a judge. He took it, however,that the cause in question relating tothe prohibitory clauses of the old N.W.T. Act contemplates the taking ofaction in this way where a vote hasbeen taken as to whether there should be licence or not. Mr. Haultain sigur-ied that such was the intention, andBennett said he thought anythingwhich would prevent these continuallysquabbles over local option would be a good thing. He agreed also that it was time the loophole whereby licenceswere secured by a man in his wife's name should be stopped. The Bill wasthe read a second time.

HAIL INSURANCE AMENDMENTS.

Mr. Haultain introduced a Billamending the Municipal Ordinance; also one to amend and consolidate the Hail Insurance Ordinance. He ex-plained that this latter Bill providedfor two changes. First, to add speltzto the list of insurable grains, andsecond, to change the charge for insur-ance from ten cents an acre, with a gratuitity for a second sum of ten centsshould the first amount not cover allthe claims, to a straight charge of fif-teen cents an acre, and also or a suralfee of fifty cents which could be paidto canvassers or agents to solicit busi-ness. During the year the main diffi-culty in connection with the Ordiancewas the small number taking advan-tage of it and it was felt that it wouldbe necessary to canvass for business.The Bill was read a second time.

Just before adjournment Mr. Haul-tain laid on the Table of the House thecorrespondence with the Federal Gov-ernment relative to the financial ques-tion and the demand of the local Gov-ernment for a larger grant.

FRIDAY, April 24th.

On the opening of the Assembly to-day Premier Haultain moved that theHouse resolve itself into committee toconsider the Supply to be granted toHis Honor the Lieutenant Governor.A message from His Honor was re-ceived submitting an estimate of $100,-000. In making the motion the Pre-mier said the government must be car-ried on until the final passage of the estimates. The estimate brought downsimply provided for a lump sum tocarry on the ordinary services of theGovernment. The Government wasasking the House to place that muchconfidence in it and he thought thecircumstances justified the course,especially as later on the Governmentwould have to ask the Houseor its approval of a detailed statement of how this money wasspent. The sum of $10,000 was alsoasked for to provide for the paymentof one-half of the sessional indemnity and travelling expenses of the Mem-oers. Both items were passed withoutdiscussion.

OTTAWA GOVERNMENT RESPONSIBLE.

In moving that when the House ad-journs to-morrow it do stand adjourn-ed until 8th June, the Premier said it was not necessary to enter into anylengthy explanation of the reasons fortaking this step. The position breiflywas that the North-West Governmenthad not been made aware of the amount of money which is to be placedat the disposal of the Territories. Re-presentations had been made to Ottawaout up to the present time no actionhad been taken, at least officially, andcommunicated to the Legislature. Onthe morning after the Assembly methe had received an unofficial telegramfrom a member of the Ottawa Govern-ment informing him in general termsof the vote which is to be broughtdown. He was asked to consider thetelegram as unofficical. The terms in-dicated in this telegram were not inthe opinion of the local Government atall suitable and the North-West Gov-ernment immediately telegraphed a strong remonstrance with a vew ofchanging the demand of the DominionGovernment. Negotiations were stillin progress. Even if they had been officially informed at that time and hadfull information it would be impossiblefor the Government at that stage ofthe session, with all the other work of the session in hand, to sit down, and work out the detailed and elaborateestimates of expenditure for the year,and especially for this year, as in viewof the storms and extra expenditurelast year the accounts were in a rathermixed condition. This was the reasonfor asking for the adjournment. He need not emphasise the inconvenienceof this state of affairs. It was suffici-ently emphasised by the fact that theHouse was absolutely obliged to ad-journ for several weeks in order toawait information which they couldreasonably claim the Legislatures shouldhave had a month or six weeks ago. The whole onus or blame, if blamethere was, for the ajournment did notrest on t he Government of the Terri-tories but belonged at Ottawa. Themotion was concurred in.

INCOMES OF $1,000 EXEMPT.

The Bill to amend the Municipal Or-dinance was also passed. These amend-ments, Mr. Haultain stated, emanatedfrom the Standing Committee onMunicipal Law. They raise the amount of income exempt from the income taxfrom $600 to $1,000. The request forthis amendment came from certaintowns where a large number of railwayemployees reside and who are also pay-ing taxes as owners of property. Itwas not the men but the municipalitiesaffected who asked for the change.

AUTONOMY AND MORE MONEYDEMANDED.

Just prior to adjournment the Pre-mier presented the report of the special committee appointed to draft amemorial to the Governor General inCouncil on the questions of the consti-tutional and financial position of the Territories, railway transportation andthe public domain. He stated that the committee had drafted two memorials,one dealing with the constitutionaland financial question and the otherwith railway transportation. Tuesdayhe read, the first being as follows :-

To His Excellency the Right HonorableSir Gilbert John Elliot, Earl of Mintoand Viscount Melguid of Melgund,County of Forfar in the Peerage ofthe United Kingdom, Baron Mintoof Minto, County of Roxburgh, inthe Peerage of Great Britain, Baron-et of Nova Scotia, Knight GrandCross of the Most Distinguished Order of St. Michael and St. George, etc., Governor General of Canada.

MAY IT PLEASE YOUR EXCELLENCY :

We, His Majesty's dutiful and loyalsubjects, the Legislative Assembly onone North-West Territories of Canada,in session assembled, humbly approachYour Excellency for the purpose ofrepresenting-

That by an Address dated on the second day of May in the year onethousand nine hundred, a copy onwhich is attached hereto, the Legisla-tive Assembly pointed out that re-peated representations had been made,in various ways, to the Government ofCanada with a view to obtaining justand equitable assistance towards pro-viding for the proper and effectiveadministration of affairs in these Terri-tories and for the public necessities oftheir rapidly increasing population, and that such representations had beenmet by intermittent and insufficientadditions to the annual grant, the pro-vision so made by the Parliament of Canada never bearing any adequateproportion by the enlargement and devel-opment of the political institutionscreated by itself ;

That by the said Address, the Legis-lative Assembly humbly prayed thatYour Excellency would be graciouslypleased to cause an enquiry to be madeinto the position of the Territories,nuancial and otherwise, and to causesuch action to be taken as would pro-vide for their present and immediatewelfare and good government, as wellas the due lumlment of the duties andobligations of Government and legis-lation assured with respect to theseTerritories by the Parliament of Cana-da, and it was furthermore humbly prayed that Your Excellency would bealso graciously pleased to order en-quiries to be made and accounts takenwith a view to the settlement of the terms and conditions upon which the Territories or any portion thereofshould be established as a Province ;

That since the passing of the saidAddress further representations havebeen made in various ways to YourExcellency's Government with regardto the financial and constitutionalposition of the Territories ;

That during the past three years the immediate necessities of the Territorieshave been vastly increased by a re-markable immigration movement,which is still going on ;

That no adequate response has beenmade to the repeated requests for the financial assistance necessary for "theproper and effective administration of " the affairs of these Territories and " for the public necessities of their" rapidly increasing population."

That the Legislative Assembly, re-presenting, as it does, the unanimousopinion of the people of the Territories,believes that nothing short of that system of government enjoyed by ourfellow citizens in the Provinces willafford a solution of the Legislative andfinancial difficulties which confront it ;

Therefore we do humbly pray thatYour Excellency in Council will causesuch action to be taken as will providefor the present and immediate financialnecessities of the Territories and willfurther provide for the establishmentof provincial institutions upon fair andjust terms analogous to those uponwhich the old Provinces have been dealt with ;

All which we humbly pray YourExcellency to take into Your Excel-lency's gracious and favorable con-sideration.

To His Excellency the Right HonorableSir Gilbert John Elliot, Earl of Minto,etc., etc., Governor General of Can-ada.

We, His Majesty's dutiful and loyalsubjects, the Legislative Assembly ofthe North-West Territories of Canada,in session assembled, humbly approach Your Excellency for the purpose of representing -

That after making due allowance for the extraordinary circumstances withwhich the Canadian Pacific RailwayCompany has had to contend it appearsto this House -

That the practically continuousfreight congestion of the last twoyears has abundantly demonstratedthat the Canadian Pacific RailwayCompany has absolutely failed to pro-vide adequate facilities for the trans-portation of the grain, cattle and othernatural products of these Territoriesand that there has resulted in conse-sequence of the lack of suck facilitiesgreat loss and damage to the people ofthese Territories not only in respect ofthe depreciation in the market price ofgrain but also in respect of cattle andhas further resulted in vexatious de-lays in the importation of merchandiseand in the transaction of the ordinarybusiness of the country :

That the volume of business origin-ating along the main line of the Cana-dian Pacific Railway and in the terri-tory directly tributary thereto neces-sitates that the energies of the Com-pany should be concentrated on the providing of adequate facilities forhandling such business (which arenot now provided) and that good faithwith the people of these Territories de-mands that branch lines already pro-mises and partially constructed shouldbe completed this year rather than


cities in territory already provided withrailway facilities and ;

That the prospective increase in the volume of traffic which largely increas-ed cultivation and settlement of landsin these Territories will certainlycreate will further tend to congesttraffic between these Territories andthe provinces to the east and unless it is held desireable to divert part of suchtraffic through foreign channels ade-quate facilities for its transportationmust be immediately provided, and

With regard to the public domainthe committee submitted a resolutionas follows :

" The believing in the justice of the claim made on behalf of any provinceor provinces hereafter established inthe Territories to the beneficial owner-ship of the public domain, this Housedoth hereby record its emphatic opin-ion against any further alienation ofthe lands, timber or minerals of the Territories except for the purposes ofhomesteading and actual settlement."

This was also unanimously adoptedand copies ordered to be sent to SirWilfrid Laurier and the Minister ofthe Interior.
