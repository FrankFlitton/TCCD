REPLY TO SENATOR PERLEY.

To the Editor of THE LEADER: Sir.-I notice in the Standard of 11 inst. that Senator Perley has been spreading himself on the subject of Provincial Automy. Now this is a subject on which the average man does not know as much as he ought to. I am an average man and Senator Per-­ley is evidently another. Nevertheless I so far agree with the Senator that the ventilation of the subject even by us may tend to throw fresh light on it. Mr. Haultain is said to advocate pro-­vincial antonomy chiefly because we cannot get sufficient money from the Federal Government to run the coun-­try properly. Mr. Perley says that if Mr. Haultain would accept the services of the North-West members and the two Senators, their influence with the Government would be so overpowering that we should secure an ample grant. Why? It seems to me that the proper party to make representations to theDominion Government is the North-West Executive, and that the influence of four party men, and two equally party Senators would be small. Mr. Perley's chief objection to our having provincial autonomy , is thefear lest we should run into debt forthe purpose of building railways andpublic works. I don't think this should alarm him. Do not most of us as private individuals run into debt for the purpose of developing our business? Probably the Senator has done so himself. The best far-­mers and business men most certainlydo so.And what, progressive and civilized country is there that has notits national debt? Take New Zealanda country which in point of populationand resources perhaps most nearlyresembles the Territories. It has its debt, but it owns its railroads and is prosperous.

Mr. Perley gets a little mixed on the subject of raising a revenue in our prospective province. In one place he says farm land is the only thing from which we could raise a revenue and that that would not amount to much because we could not sell it, in which case Mr. Perley may set his mind at rest, assured that that will not amount to much either, as no capitalists will advance money without security. But in another part of his letter Mr. Perley says minerals, forests and fish are valuable sources of wealth, but the food producing country is the greatest of all.

Perhaps so, but it follows if we accept this axiom that it will stand tax-­ation with the greater case, and pro-­duce the biggest revenue.

Cannot Mr. Perley see how inconsis-­tent it is for us without any protest from W. D. P. to allow our towns, municipalities and even school districts to raise money for public works, and yet to deny the privilege to a country the size of all the Maritime provinces put together?

To read the Senator's remarks on immigration one would suppose that he was a trade unionist, and regarded every immigrant as a possible compet-­itor and blackleg. He says “they (the immigrants) are not worth one dollar to any of the local governments." To be consistent Mr. Perley should set up a Chinese wall the Conservatives know what that is at the Manitoba boundary and not allow another immigrant to enter the Territories. In the mean-­time it would appear that every Govern-­ment and every little district goes into raptures over the arrival of every batch of these newcomers who are not worth a red cent. Strange, isn't it? I am at one with Mr. Perley in thinking that if there is to be provincial govern-­ment there should be but one province for the whole of the Territories, and while $500 may be enough to pay ourlocal members, still one cannot helpthinking that a member of the Senate who draws, S1,000, besides perquisites and travelling expenses (which he pockets). is not the man to cast thefirst stone.

As I was about to conclude this letter my eye caught the final sentence of Mr. Perley's epistle: “Then will be the time when we will want the right men in the right place.” Taking this inconnection with the Senator's farmerwild attacks on Mr. Haultain, we arejustified in assuming that on this ideahis whole article was based. Until weget the Senator's nominees in theExecutive we are to mark time. Howpleasant !

C. P. PACKINSON.Chickney, April 13, 1900.

PROROGATION.

Precisely at half past three on Fri-­day afternoon a trumpet flourish an-­nounced the arrival of Lieutenant Governor Forget to prorogue, the Assembly. His Honor entered the House, accompanied by Supt. Strick-­land, N.W.M.P., as aide de camp, and having given assent to the bills pass-­ed by the Legislature prorogued the House by the following speech:

Mr. Speaker and gentlemen of the Legislative Assembly:In relieving you of your legislative duties. I desire to thank you for the diligent considera-­tion which you have given to the mat-­ters to which your attention has been directed. I am glad to observe the unanimity with which the proposals submitted by my Government for meeting the present and immediate necessities, as well as the future posi- tion and condition of these Territories, have met your approval. I thank you for the supplies granted for the Public Service, and while regretting the sad occurrence at Ottawa and Hull, I amglad to observe that you have given practical expression to the sympathe-­tic feelings of the people of the Terri-­tories for the suffering of our fellow countrymen in those cities. I now bid you farewell in full confidence that your labors will conduce to the welfare of the Territories.

Mr. G. H. V. Bulyea, Territorial Secretary, then announced the proro-­gation and the proceedings terminated.
