THE BUDGET SPEECH.

One chief item of political interest every year is the presentation by the Finance Minister of the Budget. In beginning his task on Thursday after­noun, Mr. Fielding's first words were: “Mr. Speaker, in presenting my sev­enth budget it is once more my good fortune. to be able to submit to the House à statement of the affairs of the Dominion for a period of unexampled prosperity.” The figures which later on he submitted entirely warranted his statement. Dealing with the fiscal year ending June 30th last Mr. Field­ing was able to say that the outcome was more satisfactory than he had estimated for in his last year's budget. His estimate of levenue for the year 1901-02 was at that time $56,800,000 and of ..". $51,000,000, lºwing h probable surplus of $5,800,000. The actual expenditure was $50,759,391, and the actual revenue $58,050,790, being $5,536,088 in excess of the revenue for the previous year. The surplus for the year was therefore $7,291,398.

The chief increases of revenue over the previous year arose from Customs and there were also large increases in excise, post office and railways. One notable decrease was in the item of Douminion Lands which includes royal­ties from the Yukon gold fields, which falling-off was due to the reduction which took place in the rate of royalty. The post office showing for the year was a remarkably good one. Leaving aside the Yukon and Atlin districts, the year's post office operations showed a balance of $5,000 on the right side. Altogether the post office deficit was only $ 221 as against a deficit in 1896 of $800,000. Hemembering the expensive services extended to the Yukon and Atlin districts and the

by the Post Office, this showing made, as Mr. Fielding said, a record which the Government and Sir William Mulock particularly might point to with satisfaction. The Intercolonial Railway showed a surplus for the year of $96,820, but to find the showing of the entire Govern­ment Railway system a deficit on the Prince Edward Island roads must he taken into account which leaves the net surplus only $57,898. The expenditure on capital account amounted in the year to $10,078,638.06, which added to the ordinary revenue expenditure made a total for the of $63,970,799.86. The public debt was increased in the year by $3,349,085 which was considerably less than Mr. Fielding anticipated in his statement last year. THE CURRENT FISCAL YEAR.

Up to April lith the expenditures were $32,870,204.95; and for the whole year Mr. Fieldingestimates the expenditures at $51,650,000, a probable increase over last year of less than one million. The estimated surplus therefore is the most magnificent in Canada's history, being $13,350,000. The revenue this year will not only meet the ordinary and capital expenditures but will leave a balance of $5,650,000 to go towards reduction of the public debt.
