NATIONAL CAPITAL PLANNING

Mr. Diefenbaker:

For a copy of the architect‘s plan for the national
capital project together with all letters and com—
munications relative to the area thereof and the
estimated cost of (a) acquiring the necessary lands;
(b) removal of buildings thereon and all other
anticipated costs and expenditures.

Mr. St. Laurent: I believe there is no
objection to the motion, but I should like to
state what I understand from it, to see it
that is what the hon. member has in mind.
The architect’s plan to which he refers is
presumably the report of Mr. Greber, who
is the consultant of the national capital
planning committee. That report has not yet
been approved by the national capital plan-
ning committee, nor has it been submitted to
the government. It is expected shortly, and
as soon as it is received it will be tabled.

[Mr. Caouette.]

COMMONS

The motion refers to the area of the
national capital project. As far as the area
is concerned, there may be some confusion
between the area it was felt desirable to
designate as the national capital area for the
purpose of establishing a framework within
which planning could be carried on, and the
parcels of land which it may be thought
desirable to acquire for government build—
ings, driveways, and so forth. The national
capital area is a large region, and has signiﬁ-
cance for purposes of planning, but there is
no thought that it would ever be acquired
in totality by the federal government.

We would understand that the letters and
communications sought are in relation to the
deﬁnition of the national capital planning
area, while the estimates of costs are in rela—
tion to the lands of which the project recom-
mends the acquisition. If that is what the
hon. member wishes to have tabled, I Will see
to it that the report is prepared at the earliest
possible moment.

Mr. Diefenbaker: Mr. Speaker, what the
Prime Minister has stated represents gener-
ally the purpose of my request. I should
like to ascertain, though, if I may, when the
report of Mr. Greber may be expected.
There is a great deal of interest in it. I
understand that a committee has been sit-
ting in Ottawa from time to time in connec—
tion with the matter, and it seems to me the
time has come for the Prime Minister to
make a statement to the house and to the
country.

Mr. St. Laurent: I will communicate with
the chairman of the national capital planning
committee and tell him it is the desire of the
members of the house to have this document
at the earliest possible moment.

Motion agreed to.
