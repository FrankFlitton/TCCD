views on procedure, the provinces refused to
co-operate. As a consequence, I repeat that
the conference was a total failure, and I
believe that I have proven my point.

Today the dominion government tells the
provinces: “Very well, you contend that we
were wrong in acting as we did and in taking
the ﬁrst steps, so go ahead and suggest a
better way; tell us, in short, what you wish,
and once you have made your wishes known
we shall be prepared to discuss with you, on
even terms, the ﬁndings of these preliminary
conferences.”

May I repeat that, to my mind, this sug-
gestion reduces to a minimum the possible
recurrence of the sometimes disgraceful per-
formances which marked interprovincial con-
ferences in the past. I submit it in all good
faith to the consideration of the house and of
the government I have the honour of sup-
porting, though I am prepared to modify my
views if they should be proved wrong. I say
merely that I do not see how we can pos-
sibly go wrong if we follow the procedure
that governed the pre-confederation confer-
ences which led to the drafting ‘of the federal
statute. Through this legislation which set
up a central administration and united the
Canadian provinces, Canada became the great
nation we are now proud to call our own.

I feel there should be no fear to suggest to
the provincial premiers that they take a leaf
