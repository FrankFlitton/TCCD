72 CONFEDERATION DEBATE.

Hon. MK. HELMCKEN—Would it be better to have Confederation with no terms at all,
or with terms equal to these?

Hon. MR. \VOOD-—I ask for different terms; power to impose our own tariff, for instance.

Hon. Du. HELMCKEN—The objections of the Hon. Member are not correct. When the
Dock and Railway are built, I admit that some source of labour, and consequent interest,
will be gone, but then we must look for other interests to arise. I admit that discontent may
arise; it has frequently been so in England, when a large number of labourers have been
thrown out of employment; but I say that the Dominion and Local Governments will have the
power and the sense to remedy any such evils as they occur.

Hon. ATTORNEY.GENmnhL—I cannot fancy anything more calculated to promote perma-
nent beneﬁt than the establishment of Graving Docks. Every ship that comes in would spend

but'to some extent to the whole Colony. It the Colony goes on and increases, so must the work

the ground that the Executive are inﬂuenced by any sectional motives. I do not see why the
Hon. Member should refuse beneﬁts which come under Confederation. He would surely not
